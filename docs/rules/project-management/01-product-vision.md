# Product Vision

The **north star** that guides every decision. It describes the long-term strategic intent of the product: who it serves, what problem it solves, and why it matters.

---

## 1. Purpose

The Product Vision defines the **destination** — the ideal future state the team is working toward. It is:

- **Aspirational** — paints a compelling picture of the future.
- **Stable** — changes infrequently (quarterly/annually, not per sprint).
- **Concise** — fits in a few sentences or a paragraph.
- **Shared** — understood by every team member, stakeholder, and contributor.

---

## 2. Format: Vision Statement Template

```
For [target customer]
who [need / problem],
the [product name]
is a [product category]
that [key benefit].
Unlike [competitor / alternative],
our product [key differentiator].
```

### Example (fictional)
> For households who struggle to manage shared expenses and chores, **LIFEY** is a household management platform that keeps everyone accountable and organized. Unlike generic to-do apps or spreadsheets, LIFEY is built for shared living — expenses, groceries, recipes, and tasks in one place, with fair splits and clear ownership.

---

## 3. Key Components

| Component | What it answers |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Target audience** | Who are we building for? |
| **Problem** | What pain point are we solving? |
| **Solution** | What is the product? |
| **Value proposition** | Why should the user care? |
| **Differentiation** | How are we different from alternatives? |

---

## 4. How the Vision Is Used

- **Roadmap prioritization** — Does a feature move us toward the vision? If not, defer or drop.
- **Scope guard** — When saying "no" to out-of-scope requests, the vision is the justification.
- **Team alignment** — Every squad member should be able to recite the vision and connect their daily work to it.
- **Stakeholder communication** — A clear vision prevents death-by-feature-request.

---

## 5. Vision vs. Mission vs. Strategy

| Concept | Scope | Timeframe | Example |
|---------|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Vision** | What the world looks like with our product. | 3–5 years | "Every household runs smoothly with LIFEY." |
| **Mission** | What we do every day to get there. | 1–2 years | "Build the most intuitive household management platform." |
| **Strategy** | How we will achieve the mission. | Quarterly | "Win with AI-powered expense splitting, then expand to recipes and groceries." |

---

## 6. Principles of a Good Vision

- **User-centric** — Focused on user outcomes, not features.
- **Ambitious but achievable** — Stretch goal, not fantasy.
- **Enduring** — Not rewritten every quarter.
- **Inspiring** — Motivates the team to do their best work.

---

## 7. Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it's bad | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| **Feature laundry list** | "We will have X, Y, Z features." | Focus on outcomes, not outputs. |
| **Generic fluff** | "Be the best platform." | Be specific about who and what. |
| **No user mention** | "Build a scalable SaaS." | Always start with the user. |
| **Changes too often** | Vision rewritten every sprint. | Let strategy adjust, not vision. |

---

## 8. When to Revisit

- **Quarterly strategy review** — Check if the vision still rings true.
- **Major market shift** — New competitor, regulation, or technology.
- **Product pivot** — The core problem or audience changes.

Minor course corrections belong in the **Roadmap**, not the Vision.
